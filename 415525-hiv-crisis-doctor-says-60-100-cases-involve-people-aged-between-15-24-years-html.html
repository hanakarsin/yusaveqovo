HIV Crisis: Doctor Says 60 Out of 100 Cases Involve People Aged Between 15 and 24 Years Old

  • Data available in the country indicate coupling of new HIV infections among the group, increased pregnancies among them and increased mother to child transmission of HIV

Head of National AIDS and STI Control Programme-NASCOP Catherine Ngugi has disclosed that for every 10 new HIV infections, 60 are aged 15-24 years of age.

Speaking to Citizen TV, Ngugi said most of the victims are adolescents and young girls who engage in relationships with 'sponsors' and at the same time with their peers.

''For every 100 new infections we have in the country, 60 are between the ages of 15 to 24. For every 10 people infected in the country each year, six are between the ages of 15 to 24,'' Ngugi said.

Mingle with age mates

Ngugi said that when such people move away from that age group, they come back to their age mates because they still want to mingle and relate with their peers.

''HIV is still an issue in the country, among other sexually transmitted infections. The prevalence is 29%, but we are worried more about infertility. Between the next nine to 10 years, we shall be worried about those adolescent girls and women over infertility,'' she explained.

She said there is need for people in this age group to learn more about sexually transmitted diseases and take precautions that come with them.

''But as well, it comes much of a relationship with the older generation. They need to abstain, be faithful, use contraceptives. They should ensure that they prevent the transmissions of the virus to their partners,'' Ngugi said.

New HIV infections

She observed that the data available in the country indicate coupling of new HIV infections among the group, increased pregnancies among them and increased mother to child transmission of HIV.

''So, they are infecting themselves and passing the virus to the young ones during pregnancy or during breastfeeding, which stands at 11%. Meaning there is a need for us to look at this triple pandemic beyond the COVID-19 because this is our future generation,'' she said.

She also said that the country's demography shows that almost 70% is made of young people.

''So, 10 to 15 years from now, who will contribute to the country's GDP? If they are being infected and being limited by the virus. Maintaining one client who is HIV positive per year is around KSh 7,000 if they fail on the first line and move them to the next line, it almost triples. So it is almost KSh 21,000, which is not sustainable,'' Ngugi said.

According to Ngugi, there is a need for all the concerned stakeholders to put in place measures and prevent HIV infections in young people.
